Normal (Gaussian) Distributions

The Normal Distribution, also known as the Gaussian Distribution, is a widely used continuous probability distribution. Its fundamental importance lies in the fact that many natural populations follow a pattern that approximates the Normal Curve. Numerous phenomena—such as the age distribution of a specific animal species, the heights of adults, and various industrial measurements—are considered to be normally distributed. Normal distribution has an important feature that many probability distributions can be approximated to Normal distributions when no of random variables becomes very large.As in the case of any probability distributions, Gaussian distribution gives a probability for a given random variable.

Manufacturing Example:

Consider a company manufacturing screws of a specific diameter. While the machinery is precise, there will always be slight variations in measurement. Most screws will have a diameter very close to the target; however, a few will be slightly larger or smaller.

If we plot a histogram of these measurements, the highest frequency occurs at the center (the mean). As we move away from the center, the frequency decreases symmetrically on both sides. By reducing the class intervals to be infinitely small, the histogram transforms into a smooth, bell-shaped curve. This is the Normal Curve. By dividing the frequency by the total number of observations, the distribution is normalized to represent probability, where the total area under the curve is exactly 1. The ordinate at mean divides the whole part into two parts. The area to the right of the ordinate and that of left of the ordinate are both 0.5.

Mathematical Definition of Normal Distribution

A continuous random variable $X$ is said to follow a Normal Distribution (Gaussian distribution) if its probability density function (PDF) is defined by the following equation:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} \right)^2}$$

Where the parameters are defined as:

  • $\mu$ (Mu): The Mean or center of the distribution.
  • $\sigma$ (Sigma): The Standard Deviation, representing the spread.
  • A random variable $X$ following a Normal Distribution is typically represented as $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, where $\mu$ is the mean and $\sigma^2$ is the variance.
  • $e$: The base of natural logarithms (approx. 2.718).
  • $\pi$: The constant Pi (approx. 3.1415).
  • The curve is asymptotic to the base on either side; i.e., the curve approaches nearer and nearer to the base, but never touches.
  • The points of inflection occur at $\mu \pm \sigma$.
  • Area Distribution (The Empirical Rule):
    • $\mu \pm 1\sigma$ covers 68.27% of the area.
    • $\mu \pm 2\sigma$ covers 95.45% of the area.
    • $\mu \pm 3\sigma$ covers 99.73% of the area.
  • Additive Property: A linear function of normal variates is also a normal variate. This property ensures that the sum or difference of independent normally distributed variables results in a new variable that is also normally distributed.

The variable $X$ is defined over the theoretical range $-\infty < X < +\infty$. However, for most practical applications, the range is considered to be $\mu - 3\sigma$ to $\mu + 3\sigma$, as it encompasses nearly all possible outcomes. In this context, $X$ is treated as a Continuous Random Variable representing a single measurement, allowing us to calculate the probability of any specific outcome occurring within the Gaussian curve.

Standarized Normal Distribution

If X is a random variable following normal distribution with $\mu$ and standard deviation(variance) $\sigma$ then the variable $Z$ = $\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$ is known as the Standard Normal Variable.It is usually represented as $ \sim N(0,1)$, where 0 is the mean and 1 is the variance of the standard Normal Distributions.

When we standardize the distribution ($ \mu=0, \sigma=1 $), the complex Gaussian equation simplifies significantly. This is the version used to calculate Z-scores and probability tables:The table is used to compute the area within two values of any variable following ND.

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{x^2}{2}}$$

In this form, the total area under the curve is exactly 1, and the curve is perfectly centered at zero. The Z-Score table is useful to compute the area between two values of any variable following the Standard Normal Distribution.

For example, when $Z = 1.17$, the table value is $0.3790$. This represents the area between zero and $1.17$, written as:
$P(0 < Z < 1.17) = 0.3790$

Because the curve is symmetrical, the area is the same for both negative and positive values of $Z$. That is: $P(-1.17 < Z < 0) = P(0 < Z < 1.17) = 0.3790$.

If neither value is zero, for example $P(-1.78 < Z < 1.78)$, we must calculate the area from each point to zero and then sum them (if they are on opposite sides of the mean):

$P(-1.78 < Z < 1.78) = P(-1.78 < Z < 0) + P(0 < Z < 1.78)$
$= 0.4625 + 0.4625 = 0.9250$
$P(Z > 1.8) = P(0 < Z < \infty) - P(0 < Z < 1.8)$
$P(Z > 1.8) = 0.5000 - 0.4641 = 0.0359$
$P(Z > 1.8) = 0.5000 - 0.4641 = 0.0359$
$P(Z < 1.2) = 0.5000 + 0.3849 = 0.8849$

Key Rule for Calculations: To find the total area relative to a positive $Z$-value, consider the two halves of the curve. To compute the area less than a positive $Z$ (e.g., $P(Z < 1.2)$), you take the entire left half $P(-\infty < Z < 0) = 0.5$ and add the area between zero and your value.Conversely, to compute the area greater than a positive $Z$ (the upper tail), you take the entire right half $P(0 < Z < \infty) = 0.5$ and subtract the area between zero and your value.

Detailed Calculation Example:

A Random Variable $X$ follows a normal distribution with mean $\mu = 45$ and standard deviation $\sigma = 10$. Find the probability for an item to fall: (1) Beyond 60 and (2) Between 40 and 56.

(i) Probability beyond 60 ($x > 60$)

Convert to $Z$: $z = \frac{60 - 45}{10} = 1.5$.
$P(x > 60) = P(z > 1.5) = 0.5000 - P(0 < z < 1.5) = 0.5000 - 0.4332 = 0.0668$.

(ii) Probability between 40 and 56 ($40 < x < 56$)

Convert boundaries: $z_1 = \frac{40-45}{10} = -0.5$ and $z_2 = \frac{56-45}{10} = 1.1$.
$P(40 < x < 56) = P(-0.5 < z < 1.1) = 0.1915 + 0.3643 = 0.5558$.

Proof for The Additive Property

A significant characteristic of the Normal Distribution is that it is stable under linear combinations. This means that if you add two independent normally distributed random variables, the resulting variable is also normally distributed.

Mathematical Proof Sketch:

Given independent $X \sim N(\mu_X, \sigma_X^2)$ and $Y \sim N(\mu_Y, \sigma_Y^2)$, let $Z = X + Y$. Using Moment Generating Functions (MGF):

$M_Z(t) = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t)$
$M_Z(t) = e^{\mu_X t + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_X^2 t^2} \cdot e^{\mu_Y t + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_Y^2 t^2}$
$M_Z(t) = e^{(\mu_X + \mu_Y)t + \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_Y^2)t^2}$

Because the result is in the form $e^{\mu_{new} t + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_{new}^2 t^2}$, we conclude that $Z \sim N(\mu_X + \mu_Y, \sigma_X^2 + \sigma_Y^2)$.

Multivariate Normal Distributions

1. Key Components: Mean Vector and Covariance Matrix

In multiple dimensions, the distribution is characterized by two primary structures that generalize the univariate mean and variance.

The Mean Vector ($\boldsymbol{\mu}$)

It is perfectly fine to say $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ denotes the Mean, but it is more precise to call it the Mean Vector. In one dimension, $\mu$ is a single number. In a multivariate distribution, $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ is a vector containing the means of every individual variable:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu} = \begin{bmatrix} \mu_1 \\ \mu_2 \\ \vdots \\ \mu_n \end{bmatrix}$$
The Covariance Matrix ($\Sigma$)

It is incorrect to call $\Sigma$ (or its inverse) the "Standard Deviation."

  • $\Sigma$ is the Covariance Matrix: It contains the variances of each variable on the diagonal and the covariances between pairs of variables on the off-diagonals.
  • Standard Deviation vs. Variance: In 1D, standard deviation is $\sigma$ and variance is $\sigma^2$. In multivariate math, $\Sigma$ is the "matrix version" of variance ($s^2$), not standard deviation ($s$).

2. The Mathematical Foundation

The PDF of a bivariate normal distribution (where $N=2$) is given by:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{|\Sigma|}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}) \right)$$

For the density $f(\mathbf{x})$ to be a constant (a contour), the exponent must be constant:

$$(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}) = c^2$$

This quadratic form is the equation of an ellipse. Therefore, the contours of a Multivariate Normal Distribution are always concentric ellipses centered at the mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$.

3. What the Contours Tell You

The shape and orientation of these ellipses are entirely determined by the Covariance Matrix ($\Sigma$):

Circle

If the variables are independent and have equal variance ($\sigma_1 = \sigma_2$ and $\rho = 0$), the contours are perfect circles.

Axis-Aligned Ellipse

If the variables are independent but have different variances, the ellipses stretch along the $x$ or $y$ axis.

Tilted Ellipse

If there is a correlation ($\rho \neq 0$) between variables, the ellipse tilts. The angle represents the strength and direction of correlation.

4. Connection to Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

To physically "derive" or draw the plot, mathematicians look at the Spectral Decomposition of the covariance matrix $\Sigma$:

  • Eigenvectors: Define the direction of the principal axes (the "tilt") of the ellipse.
  • Eigenvalues ($\lambda$): Define the lengths of the axes. The width of the contour in the direction of an eigenvector is proportional to $\sqrt{\lambda}$.

5. Why this matters in Neyman-Pearson

Returning to our earlier discussion on Neyman-Pearson, if you are testing two multivariate hypotheses:

$H_0: \mathbf{x} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\mu}_0, \Sigma)$    $H_1: \mathbf{x} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1, \Sigma)$

The "Likelihood Ratio" effectively compares two sets of these contour ellipses. The Optimal Decision Boundary where the ratio is constant ($k$) will actually be a straight line (or hyperplane in higher dimensions) that cuts through the intersection of these overlapping ellipses.

6. High-Dimensional "ND" (N > 2)

If you have more than two dimensions (ND), you cannot visualize the full contour in a single 2D plot. Instead, you use:

  • Slice: Fix $N-2$ variables at their mean and plot the remaining two.
  • Projection: Project the $N$-dimensional "hyper-ellipsoid" onto a 2D plane.
  • Pair Plots: A grid of 2D contour plots showing every possible pair of variables.
