Estimation Theory and Applications

Predicting Indian elections is one of the most complex applications of estimation because the "Truth" (the actual vote of 900+ million people) is only revealed on counting day. Until then, pollsters must rely on "Snapshots" (Opinion and Exit Polls).

The Challenge: With a country as geographically and demographically diverse as India, you cannot interview every single voter (the Population). Instead, you conduct a field survey of 50,000 voters across various constituencies (the Sample) and estimate based on these samples.

In statistics, there are two widely used types of estimations:

  • (1) Point Estimate: Provides a single specific number.
    Example: "Our model predicts 285 seats."
  • (2) Interval Estimate: Provides a range and confidence interval.
    Example: "We are 95% confident the result is between 272 and 298 seats."

Example: Sample Mean is Unbiased Estimator

Context: To find the average height ($\mu$) of every adult in Kerala, we take a sample ($x_i$) at Vizhinjam Port.

The Derivation:

$$E[\bar{x}] = E\left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum x_i \right] = \frac{1}{n} \sum E[x_i] = \frac{1}{n} (n\mu) = \mu$$

This proves $E[\bar{x}] = \mu$, meaning the sample mean is "aimed" perfectly at the truth.

Likelihood Function

The Likelihood Function $L(\theta)$ is the mathematical bridge that allows us to reverse-engineer our parameters. While "Probability" predicts the data given a fixed parameter, "Likelihood" assesses the parameter given the observed data.

  • Definition: For a set of independent observations $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$, the Likelihood Function is the joint probability density viewed as a function of the parameter $\theta$: $$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i | \theta)$$
  • The "Reverse" Logic: In our Port Depth example, if we measure 17.5m, the Likelihood Function tells us how "plausible" it is that the true depth $\theta$ is 18m versus 15m.
  • Not a Distribution: Unlike a probability distribution, the area under a Likelihood Function does not necessarily sum to 1.
Finding a Sufficient Estimator:

To identify a Sufficient Statistic $T(x)$, we use the Fisher-Neyman Factorization Theorem. This requires us to write the Likelihood Function as a product of two distinct functions, $L_1$ and $L_2$:

$$L(\theta) = L_1(T(x), \theta) \cdot L_2(x)$$

  • $L_1$ (The Kernel): Contains both the statistic $T(x)$ and the parameter $\theta$. This is the part of the data that "interacts" with the parameter.
  • $L_2$ (The Noise): Depends only on the sample data $x$ and not on $\theta$. This represents information that is irrelevant for estimating the parameter.

If you can successfully factor the Likelihood this way, $T(x)$ is guaranteed to be a sufficient statistic—capturing every ounce of useful information the sample has to offer.

Example:Sample Mean is a Consistent Estimator

We want to show that the mean of a sample ($\bar{x}$) taken from a normal population is a Consistent Estimator of the population mean ($\mu$).

The Mathematical Derivation

Step 1: Properties of the Sample Mean

Given a normal population where each observation has $E(x_i) = \mu$ and $V(x_i) = \sigma^2$:

  • Expected Value: $E(\bar{x}) = \mu$
  • Variance: $V(\bar{x}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$

Step 2: Chebyshev's Inequality

We use Chebyshev's Inequality to bound the probability that our estimator deviates from $\mu$ by more than a small value $\epsilon$:

$$P(|\bar{x} - \mu| \ge \epsilon) \le \frac{V(\bar{x})}{\epsilon^2}$$

Step 3: Substitution and Limit

Substituting $V(\bar{x}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$ into the inequality:

$$P(|\bar{x} - \mu| \ge \epsilon) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{n \epsilon^2}$$

Now, we take the limit as the sample size $n$ approaches infinity:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P(|\bar{x} - \mu| \ge \epsilon) \le \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\sigma^2}{n \epsilon^2} = 0$$

As $n$ increases, the probability of the sample mean ($\bar{x}$) differing from the true mean ($\mu$) by any amount vanishes. This mathematically proves that $\bar{x}$ is a consistent estimator of $\mu$.

Example:Efficient Estimator

The Problem:

If $x_1, x_2, x_3$ are three independent observations drawn from a population with mean = $\mu$ and variance = $\sigma^2$, find the Relative Efficiency of $t_1$ and $t_2$. Which is more efficient?

  • $t_1 = 3x_1 + 2x_2 - 4x_3$
  • $t_2 = 3x_1 - 5x_2 + 3x_3$

Step 1: Check for Unbiasedness

For an estimator to be compared fairly, we check if $E(t) = \mu$. Since $E(x_1) = E(x_2) = E(x_3) = \mu$:

  • $E(t_1) = E[3x_1 + 2x_2 - 4x_3] = 3\mu + 2\mu - 4\mu = \mu$ (Unbiased)
  • $E(t_2) = E[3x_1 - 5x_2 + 3x_3] = 3\mu - 5\mu + 3\mu = \mu$ (Unbiased)

Step 2: Calculate Variances

Since the observations are independent, $V(ax + by) = a^2V(x) + b^2V(y)$. Given $V(x_i) = \sigma^2$:

For $t_1$:

$$V(t_1) = 3^2\sigma^2 + 2^2\sigma^2 + (-4)^2\sigma^2 = (9 + 4 + 16)\sigma^2 = 29\sigma^2$$

For $t_2$:

$$V(t_2) = 3^2\sigma^2 + (-5)^2\sigma^2 + 3^2\sigma^2 = (9 + 25 + 9)\sigma^2 = 43\sigma^2$$

Step 3: Determine Relative Efficiency

Relative Efficiency (RE) is the ratio of the variances. We calculate $RE(t_1, t_2)$ as:

$$RE(t_1, t_2) = \frac{V(t_2)}{V(t_1)} = \frac{43\sigma^2}{29\sigma^2} \approx 1.48$$

An estimator is more efficient if it has a **lower variance**. Since $V(t_1) = 29\sigma^2$ and $V(t_2) = 43\sigma^2$, $t_1$ is more efficient than $t_2$.

The relative efficiency of 1.48 indicates that $t_1$ is roughly 48% more efficient than $t_2$.

Example: Sufficiency of the Sample Mean

Statement: If $\bar{x}$ is the mean of a random sample drawn from a population following $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, show that $\bar{x}$ is a sufficient estimator of $\mu$ when $\sigma$ is known.

The Goal: We must factor the Likelihood Function $L(\mu)$ into two parts: $L(\mu) = L_1(\bar{x}, \mu) \cdot L_2(x_1, \dots, x_n)$. If $L_1$ depends on the data only through $\bar{x}$, then $\bar{x}$ is sufficient.

The Step-by-Step Proof

Step 1: Write the Likelihood Function

For a sample from a Normal distribution, the joint PDF (Likelihood) is:

$$L(\mu) = \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \right)^n \exp \left[ -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu)^2 \right]$$

Step 2: Use the Algebraic Identity

We use the standard identity for the sum of squares by adding and subtracting $\bar{x}$ inside the summation:

$$\sum (x_i - \mu)^2 = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 + n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2$$

Step 3: Factor the Likelihood

Substituting this identity back into our Likelihood equation, we get:

$$L(\mu) = \underbrace{\exp \left[ -\frac{n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right]}_{L_1(\bar{x}, \mu)} \cdot \underbrace{\left( \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2} \right)^{n/2} \exp \left[ -\frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{2\sigma^2} \right]}_{L_2(x_1, \dots, x_n)}$$

Conclusion

  • $L_1(\bar{x}, \mu)$: This part contains the parameter $\mu$ and the statistic $\bar{x}$.
  • $L_2(x_1, \dots, x_n)$: Since $\sigma$ is known, this part does not depend on $\mu$. It is purely a function of the sample data.

According to the Factorization Theorem, because the Likelihood can be split such that $\mu$ only interacts with the data through $\bar{x}$, we conclude that $\bar{x}$ is a sufficient estimator of $\mu$.

Key Insight for Students: This means if you know the sample mean ($\bar{x}$), keeping the individual $x_i$ measurements (the raw heights of every person at the port) provides no additional information about the true population mean ($\mu$). The mean $\bar{x}$ has "exhausted" the information in the sample.

More on Interval Estimation

The technique of interval estimation consists in the determination of two constants $a$ and $b$ such that:

$$P[a < \theta < b \text{, for a given value of } t] = 1 - \alpha$$

where $\alpha$ is the level of Significance. The interval $(a, b)$ within which the unknown value of the parameter $\theta$ is expected to lie is known as the Confidence Interval. The two constants $a$ and $b$ are confidence limits.

For example:

When $\alpha = 0.05$ and $1 - \alpha = 0.95$. Let $t$ be the sample statistic used for estimating the population parameter $\theta$. Then we have to find constants $a$ and $b$ such that:

$$P[a < \theta < b] = 0.95$$

When we are dealing with a Normal Distribution and the population standard deviation ($\sigma$) is known, the Confidence Interval estimate of $\mu$ is given by:

$$\left( \bar{x} - z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}, \bar{x} + z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \right)$$

Breaking down the components:
  • $\bar{x}$: The sample mean (our point estimator).
  • $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$: The Standard Error, which represents the standard deviation of the sampling distribution.
  • $z_{\alpha/2}$: The critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the level of significance $\alpha$.
