Estimation Theory and Applications

Predicting Indian elections is one of the most complex applications of estimation because the "Truth" (the actual vote of 900+ million people) is only revealed on counting day. Until then, pollsters must rely on "Snapshots" (Opinion and Exit Polls).

The Challenge: With a country as geographically and demographically diverse as India, you cannot interview every single voter (the Population). Instead, you conduct a field survey of 50,000 voters across various constituencies (the Sample) and estimate based on these samples.

In statistics, there are two widely used types of estimations:

  • (1) Point Estimate: Provides a single specific number.
    Example: "Our model predicts 285 seats."
  • (2) Interval Estimate: Provides a range and confidence interval.
    Example: "We are 95% confident the result is between 272 and 298 seats."

Criteria for a Good Estimator

Criteria Necessary & Sufficient Conditions Real-World Example (The "Story") Key Insight
Unbiasedness $$E[\hat{\theta}] = \theta$$ The Port Depth: An unbiased sonar reads 17.8m and 18.2m; the average hits the true 18m exactly. No systematic "offset" in the tool.
Consistency $$\lim_{n \to \infty} P(|\hat{\theta}_n - \theta| < \epsilon) = 1$$ Exit Polling: Predictions converge tightly to the truth as more voters are counted. Estimate "collapses" onto truth as data increases.
Efficiency $$Var(\hat{\theta}) = \frac{1}{I(\theta)}$$ Ship Tracking: Algorithm A has 5-min variance; Algorithm B has 20-min. A is more efficient. Achieves the Cramer-Rao Lower Bound.
Sufficiency $$f(x|\theta) = g(T(x), \theta) \cdot h(x)$$ Income Tax: You only need the sum of incomes to estimate total wealth, not individual names. Captures 100% of the useful information.

Proof: Sample Mean is Unbiased

Context: To find the average height ($\mu$) of every adult in Kerala, we take a sample ($x_i$) at Vizhinjam Port.

The Derivation:

$$E[\bar{x}] = E\left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum x_i \right] = \frac{1}{n} \sum E[x_i] = \frac{1}{n} (n\mu) = \mu$$

This proves $E[\bar{x}] = \mu$, meaning the sample mean is "aimed" perfectly at the truth.

Likelihood Function

The Likelihood Function $L(\theta)$ is the mathematical bridge that allows us to reverse-engineer our parameters. While "Probability" predicts the data given a fixed parameter, "Likelihood" assesses the parameter given the observed data.

  • Definition: For a set of independent observations $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$, the Likelihood Function is the joint probability density viewed as a function of the parameter $\theta$: $$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i | \theta)$$
  • The "Reverse" Logic: In our Port Depth example, if we measure 17.5m, the Likelihood Function tells us how "plausible" it is that the true depth $\theta$ is 18m versus 15m.
  • Not a Distribution: Unlike a probability distribution, the area under a Likelihood Function does not necessarily sum to 1.
Finding a Sufficient Estimator:

To identify a Sufficient Statistic $T(x)$, we use the Fisher-Neyman Factorization Theorem. This requires us to write the Likelihood Function as a product of two distinct functions, $L_1$ and $L_2$:

$$L(\theta) = L_1(T(x), \theta) \cdot L_2(x)$$

  • $L_1$ (The Kernel): Contains both the statistic $T(x)$ and the parameter $\theta$. This is the part of the data that "interacts" with the parameter.
  • $L_2$ (The Noise): Depends only on the sample data $x$ and not on $\theta$. This represents information that is irrelevant for estimating the parameter.

If you can successfully factor the Likelihood this way, $T(x)$ is guaranteed to be a sufficient statistic—capturing every ounce of useful information the sample has to offer.
