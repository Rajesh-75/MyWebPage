Sampling Distribution

Statistics and Parameters

A measurable, single-valued function of the observations in a sample is called a statistic. If $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$ is a sample drawn from a population, then $t = f(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n)$ is a statistic. For example: Sample mean = $\frac{x_1 + x_2 + \dots + x_n}{n}$ is a function of the sample values. So it is a statistic. Any function of the population values is called parameters. For example mean and standard deviation are parameters of the population distribution.

Sampling Distribution

A statistic is a random variable. Because the sample is random, the statistic calculated from it changes with every new sample, and the distribution of samples thus obtained is the sampling distribution. Since the sample mean is a statistic, the distribution of the sample mean is a sampling distribution.

Standard Error

The standard deviation of a sampling distribution is called the Standard Error (S.E.).

  • $\bar{x}$ has a sampling distribution. The S.D. of this distribution is called the Standard Error of $\bar{x}$.
  • If '$t$' is a statistic, then the Standard Error of '$t$' is the S.D. of the sampling distribution of $t$.
  • Mathematically, the S.E. of $t = \sqrt{E[t - E(t)]^2}$. This is analogous to the S.D. of the population.
  • In practical terms, the Standard Error is the standard deviation of the means calculated from all possible samples of the same size drawn from the population.
Note: When the population follows a normal distribution, we can study the sampling distributions even if the sample size is small. Similarly, if the sample size is large, we can study the sampling distribution even without knowing the specific distribution of the population.

Sampling Distribution of mean of samples drawn from a normal population

To derive the sampling distribution of the mean $\bar{x}$, we use the property that the Moment Generating Function (MGF) of a sum of independent random variables is the product of their individual MGFs.

Mathematical Derivation via MGF

1. The MGF of the Population

Given that $x \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the MGF of a single observation $x_i$ is:

$$M_{x_i}(t) = E[e^{tx_i}] = \exp\left( \mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2} \right)$$ 2. Define the Sample Mean

Let $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$ be a random sample of size $n$. The sample mean is defined as:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n x_i$$ 3. Derive the MGF of $\bar{x}$

By definition, the MGF of the sample mean is:

$$M_{\bar{x}}(t) = E[e^{t\bar{x}}] = E\left[ e^{t \left( \frac{1}{n} \sum x_i \right)} \right]$$

Using the properties of exponents and independence, we can rewrite this as the product of expectations:

$$M_{\bar{x}}(t) = E[e^{\frac{t}{n}x_1}] \cdot E[e^{\frac{t}{n}x_2}] \cdots E[e^{\frac{t}{n}x_n}]$$ $$M_{\bar{x}}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{x_i}\left( \frac{t}{n} \right)$$ 4. Substitute the Normal MGF

Substitute the expression for $M_{x_i}$ where the argument is $\frac{t}{n}$:

$$M_{\bar{x}}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n \exp\left( \mu \left(\frac{t}{n}\right) + \frac{\sigma^2 \left(\frac{t}{n}\right)^2}{2} \right)$$

Since we are multiplying $n$ identical terms, we raise the expression to the power of $n$ and simplify:

$$M_{\bar{x}}(t) = \left[ \exp\left( \frac{\mu t}{n} + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2n^2} \right) \right]^n$$ $$M_{\bar{x}}(t) = \exp\left( n \cdot \frac{\mu t}{n} + n \cdot \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2n^2} \right)$$ $$M_{\bar{x}}(t) = \exp\left( \mu t + \frac{(\sigma^2/n) t^2}{2} \right)$$
Conclusion: The final expression is the MGF of a Normal distribution with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2/n$. Therefore, we have proven:

$\bar{x} \sim N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$

Note: Why use MGF to derive Sampling Distributions?

When we derive the distribution of a sample mean ($\bar{x}$), we are essentially adding up $n$ different random variables. Doing this directly with Probability Density Functions (PDFs) is extremely difficult. The MGF is the preferred tool because:

Multiplication is easier than Convolution

In the "Normal" world (PDFs), adding two variables requires a mathematical operation called Convolution, which involves solving a complex integral. However, in the "MGF" world, adding variables is equivalent to simple multiplication.

  • PDF Approach: $\int f(x) \cdot f(z-x) dx$ (Hard)
  • MGF Approach: $M_x(t) \cdot M_y(t)$ (Easy)
Example: Probability and the Sampling Distribution

Question: A sample of 20 items is drawn from a normal population with mean $\mu = 30$ and $SD = 10$. Find the probability that the mean of the sample ($\bar{x}$):
(1) Exceeds 35
(2) Lies between 28 and 32.

Solution

Step 1: Identify the Parameters

  • Population Mean ($\mu$): $30$
  • Population SD ($\sigma$): $10$
  • Sample Size ($n$): $20$
  • Standard Error (SE): $$SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{20}} = \frac{10}{4.472} \approx 2.236$$

The sampling distribution is $\bar{x} \sim N(30, 2.236)$.

Step 2: Probability that $\bar{x}$ exceeds 35

We want to find $P(\bar{x} > 35)$. First, calculate the Z-score:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{SE} = \frac{35 - 30}{2.236} = \frac{5}{2.236} \approx 2.24$$

Using the Z-table, the area to the left of $Z = 2.24$ is approximately $0.9875$.

Find the right tail:

$$P(Z > 2.24) = 1 - 0.9875 = 0.0125$$

Answer (1): There is a 1.25% probability that the sample mean exceeds 35.

Step 3: Probability that $\bar{x}$ lies between 28 and 32

We want to find $P(28 < \bar{x} < 32)$. Calculate Z-scores for both boundaries:

  • For 32: $Z_1 = \frac{32 - 30}{2.236} = \frac{2}{2.236} \approx 0.89$
  • For 28: $Z_2 = \frac{28 - 30}{2.236} = \frac{-2}{2.236} \approx -0.89$

Using the Z-table:

  • Area for $Z = 0.89$ is $0.8133$
  • Area for $Z = -0.89$ is $0.1867$

Subtract the areas to find the region between them:

$$P(-0.89 < Z < 0.89) = 0.8133 - 0.1867 = 0.6266$$

Answer (2): There is a 62.66% probability that the sample mean lies between 28 and 32.

Deriving the Chi-Square Test statistic

The Rule of Transformation (Jacobian)

The Concept: If we have a random variable $Y$ with a known PDF and we define a new variable $X$ such that $Y = g(X)$, the PDF of $X$ is not simply $f_Y(g(x))$. We must multiply by the Jacobian $\left| \frac{dy}{dx} \right|$.

The Purpose: This ensures the total probability remains conserved. The Jacobian acts as a correction factor to stretch or compress the probability density so the area under the new curve still equals 1.

1. Define the Chi-Square Variable

We start with the standardized statistic $Y = \frac{nS^2}{\sigma^2}$. We know $Y \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$:

$$f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{2^{(n-1)/2}\Gamma(\frac{n-1}{2})} y^{(\frac{n-1}{2})-1} e^{-y/2}$$

2. Apply the Transformation Rule

To find the density of the actual sample variance $S^2$, we map the density of $Y$ onto the scale of $s^2$:

$$f_{S^2}(s^2) = f_Y(y) \cdot \left| \frac{dy}{ds^2} \right|$$

3. The Scaling Factor (Jacobian Calculation)

Given $y = \left(\frac{n}{\sigma^2}\right)s^2$, the derivative is:

$$\frac{dy}{ds^2} = \frac{n}{\sigma^2}$$

4. Final Result: The PDF of $S^2$

Substituting $y$ and the scaling factor, we arrive at the distribution specifically for sample variance:

$$f_{S^2}(s^2) = \frac{1}{2^{(n-1)/2}\Gamma(\frac{n-1}{2})} \left( \frac{ns^2}{\sigma^2} \right)^{(\frac{n-1}{2})-1} e^{-\frac{ns^2}{2\sigma^2}} \cdot \left( \frac{n}{\sigma^2} \right)$$

This identifies $S^2$ as following a Gamma Distribution with:

  • Shape ($\alpha$): $\frac{n-1}{2}$
  • Scale ($\beta$): $\frac{2\sigma^2}{n}$
Worked Example: Finding Minimum Sample Variance

Problem: A sample of $n = 25$ items is drawn from a normal population with a variance $\sigma^2 = 60$. Find the minimum value of the sample variance ($s^2$) such that the probability of the variance being at least this value is $0.75$.

Step 1: Identify Given Parameters

  • • Sample size ($n$) = $25$
  • • Degrees of Freedom ($df$) = $n - 1 = 24$
  • • Population Variance ($\sigma^2$) = $60$
  • • Target Probability: $P(S^2 \geq s^2_{min}) = 0.75$

Step 2: Relate to Chi-Square Distribution

The standardized statistic follows the Chi-square distribution:

$$\chi^2 = \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma^2}$$

We need the critical value $y$ such that $P(\chi^2_{24} \geq y) = 0.75$.

Step 3: Solve for $s^2$

From Chi-square tables ($df = 24$), $y \approx 19.037$:

$$19.037 = \frac{24 \cdot s^2}{60} \implies s^2 = 19.037 \times 2.5 = 47.5925$$

Result: The minimum sample variance is approximately 47.59.

Important Distinction: While we use Chi-Square here, we assume the Population is Normal.
Deriving the Test statistic for T-Distribution

A $t$-distributed variable is mathematically defined as the ratio of a Standard Normal variable ($Z$) to the square root of an independent Chi-square variable ($V$) divided by its degrees of freedom ($k$):

$$t = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{V / k}}$$

1. Let us define each components of Ratio

Assuming $S^2$ is the biased sample variance: $S^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2$.

Numerator part: The Standard Normal ($Z$)
If $x \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then the sample mean $\bar{x} \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$. Standardizing gives:

$$Z = \frac{(\bar{x} - \mu)\sqrt{n}}{\sigma}$$

Denominator part:The Chi-square ($V$)
The sum of squared deviations follows a Chi-square distribution with $k = n-1$ degrees of freedom:

$$V = \frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{\sigma^2} = \frac{nS^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{(n-1)}$$

Step 2. Construct the Ratio

Plugging $Z$ and $V$ into the $t$-definition formula:

$$t = \frac{\frac{(\bar{x} - \mu)\sqrt{n}}{\sigma}}{\sqrt{\frac{nS^2/\sigma^2}{n-1}}}$$ Which leads to $$t = \frac{(\bar{x} - \mu)\sqrt{n}}{\sigma} \cdot \frac{\sigma \sqrt{n-1}}{S \sqrt{n}}$$ $$t = \frac{(\bar{x} - \mu)\sqrt{n-1}}{S}$$

Step 4. Conclusion

Because we represented the expression as $Z / \sqrt{V/k}$ = $t = \frac{(\bar{x} - \mu)\sqrt{n-1}}{S}$, it follows the Student's $t$-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom.

Mathematical Idea." The $(\bar{x} - \mu)$ part brings in the Normal property, and the $S$ part brings in the Chi-square property. By combining them, the unknown population variance $\sigma$ disappears, allowing for inference without perfect population knowledge.
Numerical Example: Inference with the $t$-Distribution

Question: A sample of size $n=25$ drawn from a normal population has mean $\mu=20$ and sample variance $s^2=8$. Find the probability that:
(1) The sample mean ($\bar{x}$) is greater than 21.
(2) The sample mean ($\bar{x}$) lies between 19 and 21.

Note on Distribution Selection: Because the population variance ($\sigma^2$) is unknown and we are utilizing the sample variance ($s^2$), we must use the Student's $t$-distribution rather than the Standard Normal ($Z$) distribution.

Step 1: Identify the Constituent Parameters

  • • Sample Size ($n$): $25$
  • • Population Mean ($\mu$): $20$
  • • Sample Variance ($s^2$): $8$
  • • Sample Standard Deviation ($s$): $\sqrt{8} \approx 2.8284$
  • • Degrees of Freedom ($df$): $n - 1 = 24$

Step 2: Calculate the Standard Error ($SE$)

The standard error quantifies the expected stochastic variation of the sample mean:

$$SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{2.8284}{\sqrt{25}} = \mathbf{0.5657}$$

Step 3: Solve for $P(\bar{x} > 21)$

First, calculate the $t$-statistic for $\bar{x} = 21$:

$$t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{SE} = \frac{21 - 20}{0.5657} = \mathbf{1.768}$$

Consulting the $t$-table for $df = 24$, the area in the upper tail is:

$P(t_{24} > 1.768) \approx \mathbf{0.0449}$ (4.49%)

Step 4: Solve for $P(19 < \bar{x} < 21)$

Due to the symmetry of the $t$-distribution around the mean:

  • • For $\bar{x} = 21$, $t = 1.768$
  • • For $\bar{x} = 19$, $t = -1.768$
$$P(-1.768 < t_{24} < 1.768) = 1 - 2 \times P(t_{24} > 1.768)$$ $$1 - 2(0.0449) = \mathbf{0.9102}$$

Result: There is a 91.02% probability that the sample mean falls within this range.

Theoretical Note: The $t$-distribution effectively "penalizes" our uncertainty by having thicker tails. If we had used $Z$ here, our estimated probability would be slightly higher, potentially leading to an overconfident (and incorrect) conclusion.

Mathematical Proof: The Derivation of the $F$-Distribution Ratio

To demonstrate that the ratio of two independent sample variances ($s_1^2 / s_2^2$) follows an $F$-distribution, we must align it with the formal stochastic definition of the $F$-statistic.

1. The Formal Definition of $F$

By definition, an $F$-distributed random variable is the ratio of two independent Chi-square variables, each normalized by its respective degrees of freedom ($d_1$ and $d_2$):

$$F = \frac{U / d_1}{V / d_2}$$ where $U \sim \chi^2_{(d_1)}$ and $V \sim \chi^2_{(d_2)}$

2. Identify the Constituent Chi-square Components

Consider two independent samples drawn from normal populations $N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$ and $N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$. The relationship between sample variance ($s^2$) and the Chi-square distribution is:

Component 1 (Sample 1): $U = \frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2}{\sigma_1^2} \sim \chi^2_{(n_1-1)}$

Component 2 (Sample 2): $V = \frac{(n_2-1)s_2^2}{\sigma_2^2} \sim \chi^2_{(n_2-1)}$

3. Construct the Ratio

Substituting these components and their respective degrees of freedom ($d_1 = n_1-1$ and $d_2 = n_2-1$) into the definition:

$$F = \frac{\left[ \frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2}{\sigma_1^2} \right] / (n_1-1)}{\left[ \frac{(n_2-1)s_2^2}{\sigma_2^2} \right] / (n_2-1)}$$

4. Algebraic Simplification

The degrees of freedom terms ($(n_1-1)$ and $(n_2-1)$) cancel out within the numerator and denominator, leaving:

$$F = \frac{s_1^2 / \sigma_1^2}{s_2^2 / \sigma_2^2}$$

Under the Null Hypothesis ($\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2$), where we assume both samples originate from populations with identical variances, the population variance terms cancel entirely:

$$F = \frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}$$

5. Conclusion

Because the ratio $\frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}$ is algebraically equivalent to the ratio of two independent, normalized Chi-square variables, it follows the $F$-distribution with $(n_1-1, n_2-1)$ degrees of freedom.

Tutorial Insight: This proof is the reason why the $F$-test is so powerful for comparing variances. It allows us to determine if two samples have significantly different variabilities without needing to know the actual value of the population variance ($\sigma^2$), provided the populations are normally distributed.
