Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a powerful statistical technique used to reduce the dimensionality of a dataset (for instance features) while retaining as much structural variance and information as possible. It accomplishes this by transforming a set of correlated features into a new coordinate system of uncorrelated orthogonal axes called principal components.

1. Data Preparation: Standardization

The first crucial step is to standardize the raw dataset. Because PCA is highly sensitive to the absolute physical scales of individual variables, features with larger numeric ranges would naturally dominate the variance calculation. Transforming each feature ensures equal weighting.

Mathematical Transformation:
We transform the raw data matrix \(X\) to yield the standardized matrix \(Z\), enforcing a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1 for each independent feature column:
$$Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}$$
Where \(\mu\) represents the feature mean vector and \(\sigma\) represents the standard deviation vector.

2. Covariance Matrix Calculation

To understand how the features vary together, we construct a covariance matrix. This matrix captures the geometric dispersion and pairwise linear dependencies across the entire standardized feature landscape.

Covariance Matrix Formula:
Given a standardized data matrix \(Z\) of size \((N \times D)\), the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) is a \((D \times D)\) square, symmetric matrix calculated as:
$$\Sigma = \frac{1}{n-1} Z^T Z$$

Matrix Structural Anatomy:

The diagonal entries express the variance of individual features, while the off-diagonal entries represent the directional covariances between feature pairs:
$$\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \text{Var}(z_1) & \text{Cov}(z_1, z_2) & \cdots & \text{Cov}(z_1, z_D) \\ \text{Cov}(z_2, z_1) & \text{Var}(z_2) & \cdots & \text{Cov}(z_2, z_D) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \text{Cov}(z_D, z_1) & \text{Cov}(z_D, z_2) & \cdots & \text{Var}(z_D) \end{bmatrix}$$

3. Eigenvalue and Eigenvector Decomposition

By performing an eigen-decomposition on our covariance matrix \(\Sigma\), we unlock the baseline structural orientation of the data. Eigenvectors indicate the specific directional axes of maximum variance, while eigenvalues quantify the density of information stored along those respective axes.

The Characteristic Equation:
We solve for the scalar eigenvalues (\(\lambda\)) and non-zero eigenvectors (\(v\)) satisfying:
$$\Sigma v = \lambda v$$

Eigenvectors Matrix (\(V\)):

A \((D \times D)\) matrix where each column is an orthogonal unit vector \(v_i\). These establish our brand new coordinate axes system:
$$V = [v_1 \mid v_2 \mid \dots \mid v_D]$$

Eigenvalues Matrix (\(\Lambda\)):

A \((D \times D)\) diagonal matrix where each entry \(\lambda_i\) corresponds exactly to the raw variance magnitude captured by its matching eigenvector column:
$$\Lambda = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_D \end{bmatrix}$$

4. Feature Vector Selection

To compress our dimensions, we sort our calculated eigenvalues in strict descending order: \(\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \dots \ge \lambda_D\). The top eigenvectors containing the highest magnitude of variance are chosen to form our compression gateway.

Principal Components Projection Matrix (\(W_k\)):
By selecting the first \(k\) columns from our sorted matrix \(V\), we construct a truncated projection matrix \(W_k\) with a condensed dimensional shape of \((D \times k)\):
$$W_k = \begin{bmatrix} v_{1,1} & v_{1,2} & \cdots & v_{1,k} \\ v_{2,1} & v_{2,2} & \cdots & v_{2,k} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ v_{D,1} & v_{D,2} & \cdots & v_{D,k} \end{bmatrix}$$

Summary of Matrix Dimensions:

  • Original Data Matrix ($Z$): $N \times D$ (Each item (row) is a sample, and each sample has $D$ features. PCA reduces the number of features from $D$ down to $k$, while the number of samples ($N$) remains completely unchanged
  • Covariance Matrix ($\Sigma$): $D \times D$
  • Projection Matrix ($W_k$): $D \times k$
  • Reduced Data Matrix ($Z_{\text{reduced}}$): $(N \times D) \times (D \times k) = N \times k$.

💡 Deep Dive: How Do We Actually Get \(\Lambda\) and \(W_k\)?

When we solve the core eigen-decomposition equation \(\Sigma v = \lambda v\), the linear algebra solver doesn't give us matrices. It gives us a raw list of individual pairs: \(D\) eigenvalues (scalars representing strength) and \(D\) corresponding eigenvectors (vectors representing direction).

Let's use a concrete example: Suppose we have a dataset with 3 features (\(D = 3\)). Solving the equation yields 3 random unsorted pairs:

  • Pair A: \(\lambda = 0.2\), Vector \(v_A = [0.1, 0.8, 0.5]^T\)
  • Pair B: \(\lambda = 4.5\), Vector \(v_B = [0.7, 0.1, 0.7]^T\)
  • Pair C: \(\lambda = 1.3\), Vector \(v_C = [0.6, 0.5, 0.1]^T\)

Step 1: The Sorting Rule (The Foundation)

Before building any matrix, we must sort these pairs in descending order based on their eigenvalues (\(\lambda\)). This ranks the directions from most informative to least informative:

Sorted Order: \(\lambda_1 = 4.5\) (Pair B) > \(\lambda_2 = 1.3\) (Pair C) > \(\lambda_3 = 0.2\) (Pair A)

1. How We Construct the Eigenvalue Matrix (\(\Lambda\))

The matrix \(\Lambda\) is simply a structural storage locker for the variance strengths. We place our sorted eigenvalues safely along the main diagonal from top-left to bottom-right, and fill all other empty slots with 0 so they don't interfere during matrix multiplications:

H $$\Lambda = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{\lambda_1} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \mathbf{\lambda_2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \mathbf{\lambda_3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{4.5} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \mathbf{1.3} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \mathbf{0.2} \end{bmatrix}$$

Takeaway for students: This matrix is just a cleanly organized diagnostic map showing that our first new axis holds 4.5 units of data variance, the second holds 1.3, and the third holds only 0.2.

5. Data Transformation (Projection)

The final operational step is to project our original standardized observations directly onto the subspace spanned by our chosen principal components matrix.

The Essence of PCA

Why It's Unsupervised

PCA operates entirely without labeled output targets or "correct answers" ($y$). It looks solely at the internal distribution of the feature matrix ($X$) to uncover the hidden geometric structure and variance profiles of the data.

Core Functions

  • Feature Extraction: Transforms original, highly correlated variables into a smaller, optimized set of entirely new, uncorrelated Principal Components.
  • Data Compression: Lowers downstream computational demands by discarding low-variance axes while retaining the most "informative" dimensions of the data.

Common Applications

  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): Enables the projection and mapping of high-dimensional datasets into 2D or 3D coordinate planes for human visualization.
  • Noise Reduction: By focusing exclusively on axes with high variance, it naturally filters out random, low-level data fluctuations.
  • Model Preprocessing: Acts as a foundational pipeline step for other algorithms by systematically removing multicollinearity and improving training efficiency.

The Bottom Line: By mapping the data observations onto these new orthogonal axes—which capture the maximum "spread" or variance of your original features—you effectively reduce complexity while preserving the fundamental geometric relationships hidden within your dataset.
