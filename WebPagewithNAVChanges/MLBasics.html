ML Basics: Generative vs. Discriminative Models

In machine learning, supervised learning models can generally be split into two main categories based on how they approach the relationship between the input data ($X$) and the labels ($Y$).

Discriminative Models

The "Boundary" Finders

Discriminative models learn the decision boundary between classes. They model the conditional probability $P(Y|X)$.

  • Logistic Regression
  • Support Vector Machines (SVM)
  • Neural Networks
Generative Models

The "Distribution" Builders

Generative models learn the underlying distribution of the data. They model the joint probability $P(X, Y)$.

  • Naive Bayes
  • Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM)
  • GANs

Ensemble Learning

Ensemble Learning is a strategic approach where multiple models (often called "weak learners") are combined to solve a single problem. The core idea is that a collection of models will perform better, be more robust, and have less error than any individual model alone.

Primary Ensemble Techniques

Bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating)

Builds multiple independent models in parallel using different subsets of the data and averages their predictions. This helps reduce variance (overfitting).

Example: Random Forest.
Boosting

Builds models sequentially. Each new model attempts to correct the errors made by the previous ones. This helps reduce bias.

Examples: AdaBoost, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost.

Why use Ensembles?

  • Accuracy: They generally provide higher predictive performance than single models.
  • Robustness: They are less sensitive to noise in the training data.
  • Reliability: They reduce the risk of choosing a poor-performing individual model.

Think of Ensemble Learning like a "Wisdom of the Crowd" effect. While one expert might make a mistake, the average opinion of a hundred experts is much more likely to be correct.
