\ Statistical Analysis - Mean & Median

Measures of Central Tendency Series

Average (Arithmetic Mean)

An average (Mean) is a simple figure which sums up the characteristics of a whole group of figures. An average is more or less a central value around which various values cluster.

Mathematical Definition

The Arithmetic Mean is the sum of all observations divided by the total number of observations.

$$ \text{Mean } (\bar{X}) = \frac{x_1 + x_2 + \dots + x_n}{n} = \frac{\sum x}{n} $$

Computation by Data Type

Series 1

1. Individual Observations (Discrete Data)

Used when individual values are given. Simply add all values and divide by the count ($n$).

Example: Average income of 10 individuals (10, 90, 85, 103, 11, 29, 84, 15, 35, 80).

$$ \bar{X} = \frac{\sum x}{N} = \frac{542}{10} = 54.2 \text{ Rs} $$
Series 2

2. Discrete Frequency Distribution

Used when values ($x$) have associated frequencies ($f$). We calculate the product of each value and its frequency ($fx$) to find the total.

Median

Median is defined as the size of the $(\frac{N+1}{2})^{th}$ item when the items are arranged in the ascending order of magnitude.

Case 1

1. Individual Observations

For ungrouped data, arrange the values in ascending order. The Median is the value of the middle item.

$$ \text{Median} = \text{Size of } \left( \frac{N+1}{2} \right)^{th} \text{ item} $$
Case 2

2. Discrete Frequency Distribution

In the discrete case, the Median is defined as the size of the $(\frac{N+1}{2})^{th}$ item, where $N = \sum f$. We use cumulative frequency to locate this item.

$$ \text{Median} = \text{Size of } \left( \frac{\sum f + 1}{2} \right)^{th} \text{ item} $$
Case 3

3. Continuous Series

For grouped data, we first find the median class using $N/2$. Then, we apply the interpolation formula:

$$ \text{Median} = L_1 + \frac{\frac{N}{2} - cf}{f} \times c $$

Where:

  • $L_1$: Lower limit of the median class.
  • $N$: Total frequency ($\sum f$).
  • $cf$: Cumulative frequency of the class just preceding the median class.
  • $f$: Frequency of the median class.
  • $c$: Class interval width.

Mode

Mode is the value of that item that occurs the greatest number of times in a distribution.

Example: In the series $23, 35, 42, 62, 53, 35, 28, 42, 35, 23, 42, 35$, the value $35$ appears most frequently. Therefore, the Mode is 35.

Empirical Relationship

Asymmetrical Distributions

When no item appears more times than any other, the mode is "ill-defined." In such asymmetrical distributions, the mode is calculated using the empirical formula:

$$ \text{Mode} = 3 \times \text{Median} - 2 \times \text{Mean} $$
Continuous Case

Continuous Distributions

For grouped data, the mode is located within the modal class (the class with the highest frequency) and calculated as:

$$ \text{Mode} = L_1 + \frac{(f_1 - f_0) \times c}{(2f_1 - f_0 - f_2)} $$

Where:

  • $L_1$: Lower limit of the modal class.
  • $f_1$: Frequency of the modal class.
  • $f_0$: Frequency of the class just preceding the modal class.
  • $f_2$: Frequency of the class just succeeding the modal class.
  • $c$: Class interval width.

Standard Deviation

Standard Deviation ($\sigma$) is defined as the square root of the mean of the squares of deviations taken from the Arithmetic Mean. It is the most widely used measure of dispersion.

The relationship between Variance and Standard Deviation is simple:

$$ \text{Variance} = \sigma^2 $$ $$ \text{Standard Deviation} = \sqrt{\text{Variance}} $$
Individual Observations

Mathematical Formula

For a population, the Standard Deviation is calculated as:

$$ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x - \bar{X})^2}{N}} $$

Where:

  • $\sigma$: Standard Deviation
  • $\sum$: Summation
  • $x$: Individual values
  • $\bar{X}$: Arithmetic Mean
  • $N$: Total number of observations
Short-cut Method

Calculation using Assumed Mean (A)

$$ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum fd^2}{N} - \left( \frac{\sum fd}{N} \right)^2} $$
Interpretation

Why the Mean needs the Standard Deviation

The Mean gives us the location of the data, but the Standard Deviation (SD) gives us the context. One without the other can be misleading.

  • Low SD: The data points are close to the Mean. The average is a reliable representative of the group.
  • High SD: The data points are spread out far from the Mean. This suggests that the average may not be a good representation because of high volatility or outliers.

The "Consistency" Rule:

A "High SD" tells us that the "Average" is not a typical experience for most members of the group. For example:

Scenario Mean Salary SD Conclusion
Company A Rs5000,000 Rs2,000 Consistent: Most people earn very close to Rs5000000.
Company B Rs500,000 Rs25,0000 Inconsistent: There are likely very high and very low earners.
Analysis

The Relationship: Mean vs. Standard Deviation

The Mean gives the "center," but the Standard Deviation (SD) tells us if that center is reliable. As a rule of thumb: High SD means the average is not a good representative of the data.

If the SD is high, the data is scattered; the "Average" might be skewed by extreme values (outliers) and does not reflect the typical experience of the group.

Comparison Tool

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

To mathematically measure the consistency of the Mean, we use the Coefficient of Variation. It expresses the SD as a percentage of the Mean:

$$ CV = \left( \frac{\sigma}{\bar{X}} \right) \times 100 $$

Interpretation of CV:

  • Lower CV: The data is more consistent and stable. The Mean is highly reliable.
  • Higher CV: The data is more variable (less consistent). The Mean should be used with caution.
