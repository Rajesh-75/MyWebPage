Probablitiy theory and applications

The theory of probability originated in games of chance related to gambling, such as throwing dice, tossing a coin, or picking cards from a deck. Today, probability is a foundational tool in domains such as AI, Machine Learning (ML), and Deep Learning (DL).

The probability of a given event expresses the likelihood of that event occurring. It is a value ranging from 0 to 1. A probability of 0 represents an impossible event, while 1 represents a certain event. While absolute certainty (0 or 1) is rarely assigned to complex real-world predictions, these values define the boundaries of the scale. Any value in between represents a degree of uncertainty

There are four important Schools of Thought on the concept of probability:

  • 1. Classical Probability (A Priori Approach)
  • 2. Statistical Probability (Relative Frequency Theory / Empirical / A Posteriori)
  • 3. Personalistic or Subjective Probability (Bayessian Theorem)
  • 4. Axiomatic Approach to Probability (Modern Mathematical Foundation)

Probability is defined as the ratio of the number of favouring cases to the total number of equally likely cases i.e. probability of the occurrence of event A is expressed as: $$P(A) = \frac{\text{Number of Favourable Cases}}{\text{Total Number of Possible Cases}}$$

1. Relative Frequency Theory of Probability

Let us consider a random experiment and let 'A' be an event. Suppose the random experiment is repeated $n$ times. Let the event 'A' occur in $f$ out of these repetitions and fail to occur in $n - f$ repetitions. The ratio $\frac{f}{n}$ is called the relative frequency ratio of the event A. As the number of repetitions increases ($n \to \infty$), the ratio $\frac{f}{n}$ tends to a specific constant value. This limiting value is defined as the probability of the event A.

Therefore, $P(A) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{f}{n}$

2.Fundamental Probability Laws

A. The Axioms of Probability

For any event $A$ in sample space $S$:

  • Non-negativity: $P(A) \ge 0$
  • Normalization: $P(S) = 1$
  • Additivity: For mutually exclusive events, $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$

B. The Complement Rule

Essential for binary classification (e.g., Churn vs. No Churn):

$$ P(A^c) = 1 - P(A) $$
Problem: Complementary & Exhaustive Events
There are three events A, B, C. One of which must happen and only one can happen. The odds are 7 to 3 against A, and 6 to 4 against B. Find the odds against C.
1. Calculate P(A) and P(B):

Odds against A are 7 to 3 $\implies P(A) = \frac{3}{7+3} = \frac{3}{10}$

Odds against B are 6 to 4 $\implies P(B) = \frac{4}{6+4} = \frac{4}{10}$

2. Apply Exhaustive Property:

Because A, B, and C are mutually exclusive and exhaustive: $P(A) + P(B) + P(C) = 1$.

$$P(C) = 1 - (P(A) + P(B))$$

$$P(C) = 1 - (\frac{3}{10} + \frac{4}{10}) = 1 - \frac{7}{10} = \frac{3}{10}$$

3. Find Odds Against C:

Since $P(C) = 3/10$, the probability of C not occurring is $P(C^c) = 7/10$.

Answer: The odds against C are 7 to 3.

C. The Addition Rule (General)

$$ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) $$
Example: General Addition Rule
The probability that a contractor will get a plumbing contract is $2/3$ and the probability that he will not get an electric contract is $5/9$. If the probability of getting at least one contract is $4/5$, what is the probability that he will get both contracts?
1. Identify the given probabilities:
  • $P(\text{Plumbing}) = P(A) = \frac{2}{3}$
  • $P(\text{No Electric}) = P(B^c) = \frac{5}{9} \implies P(B) = 1 - \frac{5}{9} = \frac{4}{9}$
  • $P(\text{At least one}) = P(A \cup B) = \frac{4}{5}$
2. Solve for "Both" ($P(A \cap B)$):

Using the Addition Rule formula: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$

Rearranging to find the intersection:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cup B)$$

$$P(A \cap B) = \frac{2}{3} + \frac{4}{9} - \frac{4}{5}$$

3. Final Calculation (Common Denominator 45):

$$P(A \cap B) = \frac{30}{45} + \frac{20}{45} - \frac{36}{45} = \frac{14}{45}$$

Answer: The probability that he gets both contracts is 14/45.

D. Conditional Probability and Independence

Conditional Probability: $P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$

Independence: Two events are independent if $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$

Example: Successive Draws Without Replacement
A bag contains 8 red and 5 white balls. Two successive draws of 3 balls are made without replacement. Find the probability that the first drawing will give 3 white balls and the second drawing will give 3 red balls.
Step 1: Probability of First Draw (Event A - 3 White Balls)

Total balls = 13. We choose 3 out of 5 white balls:

$$P(A) = \frac{\binom{5}{3}}{\binom{13}{3}} = \frac{10}{286} = \frac{5}{143}$$

Step 2: Probability of Second Draw (Event B|A - 3 Red Balls)

After the first draw, 10 balls remain (8 red, 2 white). We choose 3 red from 8:

$$P(B|A) = \frac{\binom{8}{3}}{\binom{10}{3}} = \frac{56}{120} = \frac{7}{15}$$

Step 3: Joint Probability ($P(A \cap B)$)

Using the Multiplication Rule:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A) = \frac{5}{143} \times \frac{7}{15}$$

$$P(A \cap B) = \frac{35}{2145} = \frac{7}{429}$$

Answer: The probability is 7/429.

Real-World Perspective

Where does $P(A)$ come from?

In textbook problems, $P(A)$ is usually a given constant. However, in practice, we derive this value in two distinct ways:

Approach Definition Typical Use Case
Measured Frequency (Frequentist) Calculated from historical data. $P(A) = \frac{\text{Wins}}{\text{Total Trials}}$. Insurance premiums, factory defect rates, coin tosses.
Degree of Belief (Bayesian) A subjective estimate based on expertise, intuition, or partial evidence. Business contract bidding, weather forecasting, medical diagnosis.

Note: Modern AI often combines both. A model starts with a Prior (belief) and updates it as it sees more Empirical Data (frequency).
